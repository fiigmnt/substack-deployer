LISTEN: Fashion’s Millennial Media Crisis episode of Puck podcast

This podcast was so good I listened to it twice . Lauren Sherman, previously of Fashionista and Business of Fashion is now at Puck doing a delicious fashion industry newsletter about business, gossip and intrigue. You can sign up here. No, this is not paid.

(I’m a paying Puck subscriber, it’s worth it, and I commissioned Sherman back-in-the-day when I was working at Vogue . Sherman does great fashion reporting, and honestly, lots of other fashion business reporting is just… not great. Don’t hate on me but Business of Fashion’s quality is hit and miss, and it’s a shame that people just assume they’re the be and end all because of the title. Vogue Business is… patchy too. I read some stuff that I’m like, wow, yes we already know that was not insightful at all. Though sometimes they’re on the money.)

ANYWAY, back to this podcast: it’s about how Refinery29, if you’re of a certain era (ie my generation) used to define internet lifestyle media, particularly for women. It pioneered fun quick videos with Lucie Fink (remember her?? She’s on TikTok as a bit of a mumfluencer, what a sign of my age) and a certain headline internet speak that has permeated, well, the internet that’s still around now. Refinery29 was then bought by Vice and is now caught up in Vice’ s bankruptcy but only as a footnote, which is a sad way to see a title like that go (however do acknowledge it got caught up in its own scandals for the last few years).

I don’t want to reiterate the podcast, but interesting points that stayed with me was how despite Refinery29 developing such a distinct tone of voice, it was replicated so easily by the digital platforms of legacy publications and Sherman’s analysis of how magazines nowadays need to nichify, and not try to cater to everyone.

EXPLORE: All the various media coverage and analyses on Edward Enninful leaving British Vogue

I feel like I need to preface this by saying I do love media. I love magazines. I bought all the Vogue editions when I was in high school: British, Australian, American, French, etc. And in the same breath I will have to admit that I don’t read magazines as much as I used to. And if I’m not reading it, a former magazine die-hard who used to work in the biz, then… well, yes. We all know.

I’m not surprised that Edward Enninful is moving on from his editor-in-chief role at British Vogue . I’m assuming that he will be freeing himself up — timewise and contractually — so that he can do more freelance work which is far more lucrative, and/or pursue a more high profile career in the spotlight (some are saying Hollywood) or generally, more as a celebrity style/cultural arbiter, I’m guessing. Fashion people are funny like that. So many fashion people feel awkward or like outsiders; you were the kid in school who went to the library to read Vogue Italia and to dream of a different world, so I think they often want to ‘make it’ in a big, headline way to usurp their former, shyer selves.

Lauren Sherman again discusses this move in this episode of Puck and Jo Elvin has written about it for her newsletter,
